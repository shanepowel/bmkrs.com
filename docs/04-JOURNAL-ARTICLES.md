# bmkrs journal: ten articles

Drafts for the website, written to the voice guide. Each has a category and a related product so it cross-links into the offering. These are opinionated on purpose. A journal that hedges reads like everyone else's.

Word counts are deliberately short; these are reads, not white papers.

---

## 1. the better-told brand wins. that isn't fair.
**category:** voice · **related:** story

Here is the uncomfortable bit. The best product does not win. The best-told one does. We learned this the hard way, watching genuinely better things lose to worse things with a clearer story and a tighter logo.

You can be annoyed about it, or you can use it. We use it.

A better-told brand is not a louder one. It is one where a stranger understands what you are and why it matters in about six seconds, and where every place they touch you says the same thing. That is a solvable problem. Most companies just never treat it as one, because it sits between departments and nobody owns it.

So they out-engineer their competitors and lose to them anyway. The fix is not more product. It is finally telling the true thing well, everywhere, on purpose.

## 2. we came from delivery, not design. it shows.
**category:** studio · **related:** motion

Most brand agencies are creatives who picked up some commercial sense along the way. We are the other way round.

We spent years inside large, regulated organisations, building the teams and the delivery that made hard things actually ship. Then we got tired of watching good products lose to better-told ones, and pointed the same rigour at brand.

What does that change? We plan. We are accountable for outcomes, not just decks. We do not disappear after the reveal. And we have a low tolerance for work that looks clever but does not do a job, because we spent a long time being responsible for things that had to work on Monday.

Design studios make beautiful things. We make beautiful things that ship and keep working. That is a different promise.

## 3. naming is a positioning decision, not a creative one.
**category:** brand · **related:** launch-kit

Teams agonise over names like they are tattoos. They are not. The right name is rarely the cleverest one. It is the one that does the most positioning work with the least explaining.

The real question is not "do we love it." It is "what does it make a stranger assume before they have read a single word of copy." That is a strategy question wearing a creative costume.

A name that needs a tagline to make sense is a name doing half its job. A name that quietly pre-loads the right expectation is worth more than a year of marketing. Decide the position first. The shortlist gets a lot shorter, and a lot better, once you do.

## 4. "bold" is the most boring word in branding.
**category:** brand · **related:** brand-check

Every studio promises bold brands. Ours used to as well. Then we said it out loud and heard how little it meant.

"Bold" is what you write when you have not decided what you actually stand for. It is a feeling, not a position. It cannot be wrong, which is exactly why it is useless. A brand that is bold about nothing in particular is just loud.

Specific is harder and worth more. Not "bold," but "the only outdoor brand that admits most of its customers never leave the coast." Not "bold," but "the bank that shows you the fee before you ask." Specificity is a risk, because it rules things out. That is the point. A brand that rules nothing out has chosen nothing.

## 5. the hand-off is where brands go to die.
**category:** studio · **related:** motion

You hire a strategy firm. They write a lovely deck. It goes to a design studio, who interpret it. That goes to a PR agency, who never read the strategy. That goes to a web team, who rebuild the look from a screenshot. Four suppliers, four versions of you.

Every hand-off loses something: the context, the reason you made a call six months ago, the small decisions that made the thing cohere. By the time it reaches the customer, the brand is a game of telephone.

The whole argument for one team is that there is nothing to hand off. The person who set the position writes the words and signs off the site. We are not precious about this for craft reasons. We are practical about it, because hand-offs are where good work quietly degrades.

## 6. one journalist beats a hundred press releases.
**category:** pr · **related:** press-launch

Most PR is busywork dressed up as activity. A release goes out to a list of five hundred people, answering a question none of them asked, and lands nowhere. Then someone reports the open rate as if it were a result.

We work the other way round. Who would genuinely care about this, and what is the story that makes them care. Usually that is a handful of people, and we know their names. One placement in the right place, read by the right people, beats a hundred that nobody opens.

This is slower at the start and much faster at the finish. The story that actually fits gets picked up and repeated. The blast gets deleted.

## 7. stop measuring the numbers that flatter you.
**category:** growth · **related:** motion-plus

Every dashboard has a comfort metric. Impressions. Followers. Reach. They go up and to the right, they feel like progress, and they rarely connect to anything that pays the bills.

The harder numbers are the honest ones. What did it cost to get a real customer. How many of them came back. How long did it take someone to understand what you do. These move slower, embarrass you more often, and actually tell you something.

We are not against measurement. We are against measuring the thing that is easy instead of the thing that matters. If a number cannot change a decision you would make, it is decoration. Track the ones that would.

## 8. if your product needs a paragraph, the brand isn't finished.
**category:** voice · **related:** story

Here is a test. Ask three people who do not work for you to explain what your product does. If you get three different paragraphs, you do not have a messaging problem at the edges. You have an unfinished brand at the centre.

Clarity is not a nice-to-have you bolt on with better copy. It is the work. A product that takes a paragraph to explain will lose, every time, to a worse one that takes a sentence, because the buyer has to do less work to get it.

Getting to that one sentence is brutal. It means cutting true things because they are not the most true thing. But the sentence is the asset. Everything else is decoration hung on it.

## 9. a rebrand should keep what people already paid attention to.
**category:** brand · **related:** rebrand

The most expensive mistake in a rebrand is throwing away equity you already paid for. A colour people recognise. A shape. A line they remember. Teams get excited about the new and quietly bin the one asset that was actually working.

A rebrand is not a fresh start. It is a careful edit. The job is to find the few things that earned recognition, keep them, and rebuild everything around them so the whole thing finally holds together. Done well, it feels less like a reveal and more like the brand catching up to what people already half-believed.

If your customers do not recognise you the morning after, you did not rebrand. You started a new company and kept the invoices.

## 10. what one week of looking at your brand actually turns up.
**category:** studio · **related:** brand-check

We run a fixed, one-week look at a brand, voice and site. People expect a polite audit. What they get is usually a list of things they already half-knew and had been avoiding.

The pattern repeats. The product is better than the brand makes it look. The website is built for the founder, not the buyer. There are three different descriptions of what the company does, depending on which page you land on. And there is one obvious thing, fixable in a fortnight, that is quietly costing them customers.

None of this needs an eighty-page deck. It needs someone from outside to say it plainly, in order of what to fix first. That is the whole product. It is also the cheapest useful thing we sell.
