# brand guidelines nobody reads are not guidelines.

every company has met one: the 80-page brand book, beautifully designed, delivered with ceremony, opened twice. meanwhile the actual brand drifts, because the people making things day to day, the marketer in canva, the developer picking a button colour, the founder writing a linkedin post, were never going to consult a pdf the size of a novella.

brand guidelines have one job: to make the next thing someone makes look and sound like the brand. judged by that job, most fail.

## guidelines fail by being complete

the instinct is to document everything. the result is that nothing is findable, so people guess. good guidelines are ruthlessly prioritised: the ten rules that prevent ninety percent of the drift, stated plainly, with examples of right and wrong. the logo misuse page matters less than "this is how we write a headline" because headlines get written daily and logos get redrawn never.

## what the useful version contains

**the one-line brand idea**, so every decision has a reference point. **voice rules with real examples**: three before-and-after rewrites teach tone faster than three pages describing it. **the banned list**: words, visual clichés and layouts the brand never touches. **the core kit**: logo files, colours with codes, type with weights, and where to get them. **a do-this-when page**: the five most common jobs (social post, deck slide, email, product screen, press quote) with a worked example of each.

that is maybe twelve pages. everything else is an appendix.

## make them live where the work happens

a pdf in a drive folder is where guidelines go to be forgotten. put the rules where the decisions get made: a notion page that is the first onboarding link, figma libraries with the components pre-built, a deck template that makes the right layout the lazy option. the best guideline is one nobody has to remember because the tooling enforces it.

## the test

hand your guidelines to someone who joined last week and ask them to make a social post. if they produce something on-brand in fifteen minutes without asking a question, the guidelines work. if they come back with "which logo do i use?", you own a very expensive pdf.
