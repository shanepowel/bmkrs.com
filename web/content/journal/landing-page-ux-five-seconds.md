# landing page ux: you have five seconds, spend them well

a visitor arriving on your landing page gives you about five seconds of genuine attention before deciding whether to invest more. in that window they are answering three questions, mostly subconsciously: what is this, is it for me, and what am i supposed to do? a landing page is good ux to exactly the degree it answers those three fast. everything else is decoration.

here is where the five seconds usually get wasted, and how to win them back.

## one page, one job

the highest-converting landing pages do a single thing. one offer, one audience, one action. the moment a page serves two goals, sign up and book a demo and read the blog, the visitor inherits a decision they did not come to make, and the easiest decision is always to leave. if a campaign has two messages, it needs two pages. this is the cheapest conversion fix that exists.

## hierarchy is the interface

eye-tracking has been telling us the same thing for twenty years: people scan in an f-pattern, read headlines and skip paragraphs, and decide from the visual hierarchy what matters. so the hierarchy has to carry the argument by itself. read only your headlines, top to bottom: if they tell the full story, claim, proof, how, who, action, the page works for scanners, which is everyone. if the argument lives in the body copy, it lives where nobody goes.

## speed is ux, and it is conversion

every second of load time costs conversions, and most landing pages are slowed by exactly two things: oversized images and script bloat from tracking tools nobody audited. compress the images, lazy-load below the fold, and delete the tag manager archaeology. a page that paints in under two seconds on a phone has already beaten half its competitors before a word is read.

## friction at the moment of action

the call to action area is where pages quietly die. forms asking for six fields when the business needs two. buttons labelled "submit" instead of saying what happens next. no reassurance at the point of commitment, what it costs, how long it takes, what you will not do with the email. every field removed and every doubt answered at the button is measurable money.

## design and message, one decision

the deeper pattern in all of this: landing page ux fails when design and copy are made separately. hierarchy is the message; the button label is the offer. we build pages with the writer and designer in the same sprint, inside our [storefront](/services#storefront) work, and we set one primary metric per page so "did it work" has an actual answer.
